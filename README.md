## Overview

The sphinx is one of the most enduring mythological composite creatures, appearing in both Egyptian and Greek antiquity with significantly different characteristics. In Egyptian tradition, the sphinx is typically a benevolent guardian — a male-headed leonine figure (androsphinx) representing the pharaoh's power, wisdom, and divine protection. Egyptian sphinxes are wingless and guard temples and processional routes; the most famous example is the Great Sphinx of Giza (c. 2500 BCE), approximately 73 meters long and 20 meters high, carved from the living limestone of the Giza Plateau, believed to bear the likeness of Pharaoh Khafre. The Egyptian tradition also includes ram-headed (criosphinx) and hawk-headed (hieracosphinx) variants. The Greek sphinx is an entirely different figure: female, winged, with the body of a lion, the head of a woman, and the wings of an eagle; sometimes with a serpent's tail. She was a deadly monster guarding the approach to Thebes who posed a riddle to travelers — 'What walks on four legs in the morning, two at noon, and three in the evening?' — and killed those who failed to answer correctly. Oedipus famously answered correctly ('a human being, who crawls as an infant, walks upright as an adult, and uses a staff in old age'), causing the sphinx to kill herself. The sphinx figure spread from Egypt and Greece throughout the ancient Mediterranean world, into South and Southeast Asian traditions, and was revived extensively in European Renaissance and later art and architecture.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: The Greek word Σφίγξ (Sphinx) is of uncertain but Greek origin, possibly connected to sphingein (to squeeze, to bind tight), referring to the lion's method of killing prey by constriction of the throat. An alternative theory proposes corruption from the Egyptian shesep-ankh ('living image'), a term applied to royal sphinx statues.
- **Meaning**: The 'strangler' or 'throttler' in the Greek interpretation; 'living image' in the proposed Egyptian etymology.
- **First use**: Egyptian sphinxes are attested from at least the Old Kingdom (c. 2600–2500 BCE). The Greek sphinx tradition is attested in art and literature from at least the 6th century BCE.

## Theories & Explanations

### Scientific

**Water weathering / pre-Dynastic origin (Schoch hypothesis)** _(Robert M. Schoch (Boston University), John Anthony West)_

Geologist Robert Schoch argued in 1992 that the pattern of erosion on the body of the Great Sphinx is consistent with heavy rainfall weathering rather than wind/sand erosion, suggesting it was carved during a wetter period in Egypt's history — potentially as early as 7000–5000 BCE, long before Khafre's reign.

### Skeptical

**Conventional Egyptological dating (Khafre, c. 2500 BCE)** _(Egyptological mainstream, Zahi Hawass, Mark Lehner)_

The overwhelming consensus of Egyptologists dates the Great Sphinx to the reign of Pharaoh Khafre (c. 2558–2532 BCE) based on context (proximity to Khafre's pyramid), ancient inscriptions, and stylistic analysis of the face. The Schoch water weathering hypothesis is disputed on geological grounds.
